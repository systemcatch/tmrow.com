A pragmatic guide to Climate Change

A very short introduction

Global temperatures are rapidly increasing

"Warming of the climate system is unequivocal, and since the 1950s, many of the observed changes are unprecedented over decades to millennia", writes the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) in its fifth assessment.

Source: NASA's Goddard Institute for Space Studies (GISS) (data)

It is very clear that the global average temperature is increasing as sixteen of the 17 warmest years have all occurred since 2001 (with the exception of 1998). Furthermore, NOAA reported in August 2016 that each of the last 16 months were the warmest they've ever measured. The measured warming has been found to be consistent across seasons and regions.

We have now reached a warming of 1°C since the end of the 70s. A change of 1°C in 50 years is unprecedented, as it can be seen from the following reconstruction of temperatures in the last 10 000 years:

What is worrying is not so much the change itself, but rather the speed at which this change is taking place. Historically, a change of 1°C seems to happen in thousands of years - not decades. The Earth is a complex ecosystem, and disturbing it by as little as a couple of degrees in the blink of a decades (or even centuries) is a non negligible disturbance.

Climate change is caused by greenhouse gases

Some gases are transparent to the sun's rays heating the Earth, but are opaque (acting as mirrors) with respect to rays emitted back from the Earth. Those gases act as a blanket, trapping heat that is unable to escape, and are called greenhouse gases due to warming behaviour. The most abundant greenhouse gas is water wapor and is part of the natural stability of our climate. By trapping heat inside the atmosphere, it enables our nights to be relatively warm compared to the cold of outer space.

By drilling Ice Cores (columns of ice containing small bubbles of old trapped air), we have been able to infer historical CO2 concentration. This concentration has been relatively stable until the industrial revolution. Again, what is worrying is the speed at which the change of concentration is happening. Both the concentration level and the speed of change are unprecendented in the history of our measurements, going back 800 000 years BC:

Source: National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) Ice Core records (data) before 1959 and Mauna Loa records (data) after 1959.

By looking at the heating and cooling effect (called radiative forcing) of other processes between now and the pre-industrial period (where the greenhouse gas atmospheric levels had not yet increased), it becomes clear that greenhouse gases are the main driver behind the observed warming:

Source: Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) WG1 AR5, Fig. 8.15 p697 (data)

The major increase in greenhouse gas emissions during the industrial revolution has therefore been found to be the main driver behind temperature increases. Several greenhouse gases exist aside from CO2, differing by how much radiation they trap and by how long they stay in the atmosphere. To be able to compare them, the so-called global warming potential of each gas is used as reference, being the amount of heat trapped by a certain mass of gas compared to a similar mass of CO2 over 100 years. This enables us to define the mass unit of grams of CO2 equivalent: gCO2eq. However, even though nitrous oxide (N2O) is worth 300 times its weight of CO2, the staggering amount of CO2 in the atmosphere makes it by far the main contributor to the greenhouse effect.

Greenhouse gases are the result of human industrialisation

The greenhouse effect has been identified since the beginning of the century. This article from 1912 is one of the earliest accounts which identifies the risk of releasing greenhouse gases, like carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere, causing an increase in temperature. Specifically, it links the burning of fossil fuels to an increase in temperature.

The invention of an efficient steam engine by James Watt in 1784 meant humans were able to effectively convert fossil fuels into energy, a process that releases carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere. Fossil fuels have a tremendous amount of energy stored. In terms of X, 1L of oil represents Y, Z. The discovery of the use of fossil fuels propelled humanity into an industrialised age, and enabled us to have the living standard we have now.
